WHAT WE LEARNED TODAY


After reviewing this chapter we learned about high-frequency activity (HFA).

HFA is an activity in the 50-150 Hz range that can be consideredas a biomarker of "active" brain areas. That is, when a particular brain area is working or processing or active in some way, HFA increase. For example, if the motor cortex is active, HFA will increase in the precentral area.

The main advantage of HFA is that it is able to identify brain areas with high-level complex functions. Remember that the primary motor cortex can be mapped with regular intracranial mapping because when stimulated a movement is provoked or inhibited. However, how to map complex language processing or mathematical calculations? HFA are able to map essentially any brain function no matter how complex.

An interesting concept brought up by this chapter is: are areas that we map as silent really silent? For example, the default mode network is probably very important because it is active most of the time, but cannot really be mapped with regular intracranial monitoring. Similarly, brain areas for mathematical calculations or complex planification are problably important but cannot be mapped with regular intracranial monitoring. Are we resecting important brain areas because we just cannot map them yet?
